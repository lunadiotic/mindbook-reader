## A Serving of Calm at the Shallow End of the Pool

### **Part 1: The Flavors We Season Ourselves**

Have you ever thought that everything happening in this world, at first, doesn't actually have any flavor?

The events that greet us today—whether it’s bad news, traffic on the road, or the fleeting gaze of a stranger—are actually just a heap of bland data. Like a bowl of plain noodles before the seasoning is added. It isn't salty, it isn't sweet, and it isn't spicy yet.

We are the ones holding the seasoning bottle. Our bodies and minds have full control to blend meaning into it.

When a tragedy or a heavy burden comes our way, we always have a choice in our heads: do we want to season it into a sadness that drains our tears, turn it into something funny that we can laugh about later, or change it into an anger that burns us from the inside out? Something becomes terrifying not because of its true form, but because of the meaning we choose to give it.

### **Part 2: The Noise in Our Heads and the Unexplored Paths**

I know there are days when your chest feels so tight, and your head suddenly becomes deafeningly noisy with something called anxiety.

But please know, anxiety is a deeply quiet and subjective thing. What makes you tremble might look like an ordinary day to someone else. And vice versa. More often than not, anxiety loves to knock on the doors of our hearts when we are facing something for the very first time. Something foreign, a path we have never walked before.

When we don’t know what lies ahead, our minds tend to guess the worst possible outcome. It’s completely human.

However, there is a simple way to get through it. Not by forcing yourself to suddenly become strong or blindly jumping into the dark, but by gently shifting your body—inch by inch—from "freezing and overthinking" to "taking a step, even if it's just a fraction of an inch."

### **Part 3: Learning to Float Without Drowning**

Let’s look back at a story about someone learning how to swim for the very first time.

All along the way to the pool, their head was a chaotic mess. It was so loud. Fears were bouncing around inside: _What if I can't do it? What if I drown? What if I run out of breath and there is no one there to save me?_ That fear made the road feel so much longer and more intimidating than it actually was.

But the moment they arrived, reality turned out to be far kinder than the monsters in their head. The swimming pool wasn't some dark, bottomless ocean. There were options. There was a very shallow pool, a wider one, and even warm water waiting for them to rinse off.

Since the goal was to learn, the most honest choice at that moment certainly wasn't to turn around and go home. They chose the most sensible path for a beginner: walking toward the shallowest end.

They didn't dive right in. They simply tried to glide their body across water that didn't even reach their waist. And then, a small miracle happened. The moment their skin felt the coolness of the water, a spark of joy slowly took over. Their focus shifted from "the fear of drowning" to "the simple pleasure of moving through water." The anxiety that had chained their ankles on the way there suddenly evaporated into thin air.

### **Part 4: Embracing the Edge**

Once the quiet pride of mastering the shallow pool began to bloom, a tiny spark of courage emerged. A soft curiosity whispered, _what would it feel like to try the slightly deeper pool?_

Of course, they weren't foolish enough to rush over and plunge right into the middle of the deep end. That wouldn't be brave; it would be reckless.

The step they took was incredibly gentle. They walked along the very edge of the pool. Lowering their feet one by one with utmost care, while both hands tightly gripped the metal railing built into the pool wall.

They started to glide, but their body never strayed far from the safety of the perimeter. They knew exactly that if their legs grew tired or their breath caught in their throat, all they had to do was reach out just a little bit to grab that railing, pull themselves back to the surface, and take a deep, safe breath.

Right there at the edge of the pool, while holding onto that secure boundary, they practiced kicking. They adjusted their body's rhythm, until they finally understood how to simply stay afloat.

### **Epilogue: We Only Need to Walk to the Edge**

In the end, making peace with overwhelming fear and anxiety isn't about forcing ourselves to be a hero who leaps directly into the heart of a deep, raging storm.

It’s about giving ourselves the grace to admit that we are scared, and then choosing to walk toward the shallowest end of the pool. It is perfectly okay if you have to start at a painfully slow pace. And it is completely fine if, the entire time, you need to hold on tightly to the safety railing at the edge—until you are genuinely ready to let your body float on its own.

You don’t have to know how to swim today. Just walk with me to the edge, and we will find a safe place to hold onto, together.

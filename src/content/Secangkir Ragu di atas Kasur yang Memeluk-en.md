## A Cup of Doubt in the Bed's Embrace

### **Part 1: We Aren't Avoiding the Work, We Are Avoiding the Feeling**

Have you ever found yourself staring at a task or a plan, only to close it right back up and say, _"I'll do it later"_?

Many people think that procrastinating—or _procrastination_—is just a form of laziness. But if we sit down quietly and are completely honest with ourselves, we’ll realize that accusation is wrong. We don't postpone things because we hate the work.

What we are actually avoiding is the uncomfortable feeling that comes with it.

Suddenly, a loud noise erupts inside our head. Doubts about whether we're capable enough, uncertainty about where to even begin, down to the deepest fear of all: _"What if the result turns out terrible and I become the subject of gossip in someone else’s group chat?"_ This fear of other people's judgment often acts like an emergency brake. It stops our body right before we even take a step, locking us in a loop of anxiety that leaves us choosing to do absolutely nothing.

### **Part 2: Hiding from the Place Meant to Heal**

Ironically, most of that anxiety is born from the giant expectations we build all by ourselves inside our heads—something that has never even crossed anyone else's mind.

Imagine someone wanting to go to the gym to shape their body into something more ideal. Yet, every time they get ready, their footsteps suddenly halt at the front door. They feel embarrassed. They're afraid of being ridiculed because they feel their body isn't proportional right now—either too skinny or too heavy.

And yet, isn't the gym precisely created and built for bodies in that exact condition?

Then, that reluctance grows even heavier when they imagine the sheer number of new machines they don't know how to use. There's a terrifying fear of looking foolish or doing it wrong in front of a crowd.

In reality, the world never watches us that closely. The people in there are usually far too exhausted and occupied with their own workout routines to even glance our way. In fact, it's not impossible that someone who is already in great shape might willingly walk up to us, smile, and show us how to use the machine. Why? Because they, too, once stood in that exact same spot: a confused beginner on day one.

### **Part 3: A Flawed Melody in the Creator's Ears**

Anxiety rooted in our own idealism really is that powerful. It has a way of turning something beautiful into something dreadful in our own eyes.

Take the story of a guitarist trying to compose a new melody. After finishing the piece, the guitarist sighs in disappointment. To his ears, the melody sounds bland, lacking detail here, and missing grandeur there. Yet, when his fans hear that very same melody, they are completely captivated and think of it as a beautiful masterpiece.

Is either one of them lying? Not at all. Both are right, it's just that they are using entirely different measuring sticks.

A fan compares that melody to an instrument that otherwise sits silent and lifeless. To someone who doesn't know how to play it, any sound born from it feels like a cool piece of magic.

Meanwhile, the musician doesn't compare his melody to silence. He compares it to the image of a "perfect and ideal masterpiece" already echoing inside his mind. Because his point of comparison is something unrendered and flawless, anything he actually manages to create in the real world will always look incomplete and flawed.

### **Epilogue: Lowering the Standards in Our Heads Just a Little**

In the end, refusing to start because we're afraid the outcome won't be perfect is the most effective way to kill our own potential.

We need to learn to give our hearts some space and accept that it’s okay if our first step looks clumsy, or if our first melody sounds simple. The fear of being judged by others is often just the shadow of our own inflated ego.

This world is far too busy with its own affairs to bother gossiping about our tiny mistakes in their group chats. And even if they do, it is completely out of our control.

You don’t have to become an expert or achieve an ideal body overnight. Just give yourself permission to be a beginner who is allowed to make mistakes. Let’s lower that ideal standard just a fraction, and then take a step—even if it’s just to use one machine wrong in the corner of the room.
